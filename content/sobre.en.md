The Clemenzo family comes from **Riddes** and **Ardon**, two neighboring villages in the Rhône valley, in the Swiss canton of Valais. The surname is documented there from 1481 onwards. In 1873, Francisco Clemenzo —my great-great-grandfather— boarded a ship bound for Buenos Aires: he was fifteen years old, had recently lost his father, and the family farm had already been sold at auctions following the family's financial ruin. He reappears thirteen years later in the Swiss colonies of Entre Ríos, married to the daughter of other Valaisans. The entire Argentine branch descends from him.

This site is the archive of that research. I have been conducting it myself, Matías Clemenzo, for years now, gathering digitized Swiss censuses, 19th-century newspapers, parish and civil records from two continents, and scattered family memory. It is not a finished tree but an ongoing work: there are facts proven by documents, approximate dates, hypotheses, and open questions. It gets updated each time something new appears.

## How to read the site

The **[Tree](arbol.html)** is the heart of the project and the best entry point. It displays the family centered on one person; clicking on any card reorganizes the tree around that person. Each person has three tabs: their vital data, their research notes (hypotheses, sources, pending tasks), and their documents. It works on mobile, but the full experience is on tablet or computer.

The **[Archive](archivo.html)** is the tabular view of the same thing: all people with their photos and documents, searchable by name and filterable by branch. The **[Blog](blog.html)** tells the story — what document appeared, what hypothesis fell apart, what was concluded and with what sources. And **[Sources](fuentes.html)** lists the archives and institutions where everything comes from, with links when available.

Each person in the tree carries a label that indicates how much is known about them:

| Status | What it means |
|---|---|
| Verified | Their vital facts are backed by a document or two independent primary sources |
| In progress | There is research material: documents, records, or cited sources |
| Incomplete | Some dates or places are known, without documentary support yet |
| No data | Only the name and family relationships |

Approximate dates are shown with `~`, and when only a range is known (for example, a death occurring « between 1851 and 1861 »), it is indicated this way, without inventing precision.

## Research status

At the time of writing, the tree has **93 people** in six branches — Clemenzo, Stalder, Roch, Queipo, Benegas, and Arceo — and a collection spanning from the Valaisan censuses of 1802 to Argentine records of the 20th century. On the Swiss side, the main sources are the digitized censuses of Valais (1802–1880), historical press (the *Gazette du Valais* proved to be a goldmine: court notices, auctions, inheritances) and the collections of the Valais State Archives. On the Argentine side, the records of Entre Ríos, the national censuses of 1869 and 1895, and military records. Part of the 19th-century findings were confirmed by cross-checking data with Jean-Yves, a distant cousin living in Switzerland who descends from the branch that did not emigrate.

The questions that guide the research today:

- **The gap 1873–1886.** Between Francisco's departure and his reappearance married in Entre Ríos, there are thirteen years without a single document. Where was he? Did he arrive directly at the colony?
- **Where exactly Francisco was born.** The tree says Riddes from the 1870 census, but in 1858 the family still had their farm in Salins. The baptism record is missing.
- **The « Lambiel cousins ».** In 1904 some cousins from Riddes managed the liquidation of the last Swiss assets of the family. I know that the mother of these Lambiels was a Clemenzo — still need to prove which of the two candidates in the clan.
- **Jeanne, the one who stayed.** Francisco's aunt continued living in Riddes at least until 1880, unmarried and with her own home. I don't know when she died, and her inheritance could explain the money that arrived in Argentina in 1905.

If you got here because a surname, a village, or a photo sounds familiar to you —or if you found an error—, write me from [Contribute](colaborar.html). Much of what is on this site started with a stray fact that someone took the trouble to send me.
